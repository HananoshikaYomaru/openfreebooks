You already know how to count and work with whole numbers from 0 to 10. On a number line, those values lie on or to the right of zero. Numbers can also lie to the left of zero — we call them negative. Temperatures below freezing, bank overdrafts, and positions below sea level are everyday examples of directed numbers.

The number line

On a horizontal number line, moving right means the value increases; moving left means it decreases. The distance from zero is the absolute value \(|a|\). For example, \(|-5| = 5\) and \(|3| = 3\).

Comparing

\(a > b\) means \(a\) lies to the right of \(b\). Any negative number is less than any positive number.

Adding and subtracting

Think in terms of movement on the line:

  • \(a + b\) — start at \(a\), move \(b\) units (right if \(b > 0\), left if \(b < 0\)).
  • \(a - b\) — start at \(a\), move \(-b\) units (equivalently, add the opposite of \(b\)).

Subtracting a negative is adding a positive: \(5 - (-3) = 5 + 3 = 8\).

Multiplying and dividing

Signs Result
positive × positive, negative × negative positive
positive × negative (either order) negative

Ratios

A ratio compares quantities of the same kind. The ratio \(2:3\) means “2 parts to 3 parts” — five equal parts in total. To share \(40\) in the ratio \(2:3\), one part is \(40 \div 5 = 8\), so the shares are \(16\) and \(24\).

Sharing in ratio \(m:n\)

\[ \text{one part} = \frac{\text{total}}{m+n}, \quad \text{first share} = \frac{m}{m+n}\times\text{total}, \quad \text{second share} = \frac{n}{m+n}\times\text{total} \]

Rates

A rate compares different units. Speed is distance per time: \[ \text{speed} = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}. \] A unit rate tells “how much per one” — for example \(60\) km in \(1.5\) h gives \(60 \div 1.5 = 40\) km/h.
