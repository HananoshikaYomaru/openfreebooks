History

For much of history, mathematicians treated only positive quantities as numbers. Negative solutions to equations were often rejected as meaningless. Practical needs — debts, losses, and temperatures below zero — still pushed people to work with “numbers less than nothing.”

In India around the 7th century, Brahmagupta gave rules for operating with positive and negative quantities (fortunes and debts). Chinese scholars used counting rods with two colours to represent opposite signs. European acceptance grew slowly; even in the 18th century some writers called negative roots “false.”

Ratios and proportions appear in Babylonian and Egyptian problems (sharing grain, scaling recipes). The modern notation \(a:b\) and systematic treatment of unit rates became standard as commerce and science needed comparable measures — speed, price per item, and exchange rates between currencies.

Derivation

Why \((-)\times(-) = (+)\)?

One useful pattern: multiplication should keep the rules of arithmetic consistent. For any real \(a\), you want \(a \times 0 = 0\). Then \[ a \times 0 = a \times (1 + (-1)) = a\cdot 1 + a\cdot(-1) = a + a\cdot(-1). \] So \(a\cdot(-1) = -a\). Now apply that twice: \[ (-a)\cdot(-1) = -((-a)) = a, \] which shows that multiplying two negatives gives a positive.

Sharing in the ratio \(m:n\)

Suppose a total \(T\) is split into two parts in the ratio \(m:n\). Think of \(m+n\) equal parts. Each part has size \[ \frac{T}{m+n}. \] The first quantity gets \(m\) of those parts: \[ \frac{m}{m+n}\,T, \] and the second gets \(\frac{n}{m+n}\,T\). The parts add to \(T\), as required.

Unit rate

If \(d\) units of quantity are produced in \(t\) units of time, the average rate is \(d/t\). “Per one hour” means divide by \(t\) when \(t \neq 0\): \[ \text{rate per unit time} = \frac{d}{t}. \] Re-arranging gives \(d = \text{rate} \times t\), the formula used for uniform motion.

Applications

Temperature and altitude

A weather station records \(3\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\) at noon and the temperature drops \(8\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\) by midnight. The change is \(-8\), so the midnight temperature is \[ 3 + (-8) = -5\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}. \] Altitude above sea level is positive; below sea level (e.g. Dead Sea shore) is negative elevation relative to sea level.

Sharing and mixtures

Paint is mixed in the ratio blue : white \(= 2:5\). For \(21\) litres total, there are \(7\) equal parts, each \(3\) litres, giving \(6\) L blue and \(15\) L white.

Speed and exchange rates

Walking \(6\) km in \(1.5\) h gives speed \(\frac{6}{1.5} = 4\) km/h. An exchange rate of \(1\) USD \(=\) \(7.8\) HKD means \(100\) USD converts to \(780\) HKD by multiplying by the unit rate.

Question bank

Work each question, then open the solution to check your reasoning. Difficulty increases through the sets.

Easy

Which is larger, \(-3\) or \(-7\)?

Answer: \(-3\) is larger (lies to the right on the number line).

Explanation: For negative numbers, the one closer to zero is greater.

Evaluate \((-2) + 9\).

Answer: \(7\).

Steps: Start at \(-2\), move 9 units right.

\((-4) \times 6 = \) ______

Answer: \(-24\).

Explanation: Different signs → negative product.

Intermediate

Evaluate \(5 - (-3) \times 2\).

Answer: \(11\).

Steps: \((-3)\times 2 = -6\); then \(5 - (-6) = 5 + 6 = 11\).

Divide \(84\) in the ratio \(2:5\).

Answer: \(24\) and \(60\).

Steps: \(7\) parts; each part \(84 \div 7 = 12\); shares \(24\) and \(60\).

A cyclist goes \(45\) km in \(3\) h. What is the average speed?

Answer: \(15\) km/h.

Calculation: \(45 \div 3 = 15\).

Difficult

The temperature was \(-2\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\) and fell by \(9\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\). Find the new temperature.

Answer: \(-11\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\).

Steps: \(-2 + (-9) = -11\).

Put in order from smallest to largest: \(-1\), \(0\), \(-\frac{1}{2}\), \(-2\).

Answer: \(-2 < -1 < -\frac{1}{2} < 0\).

Explanation: On the number line, further left means smaller.

Hardcore

\(A:B = 3:4\) and \(B:C = 2:5\). Find \(A:B:C\) in simplest form.

Answer: \(A:B:C = 3:4:10\) after matching \(B\) (multiply first ratio by 2: \(6:8\), second by 4: \(8:20\) → \(6:8:20 = 3:4:10\)).

Steps: Align \(B\) to LCM 8: \(A:B = 6:8\), \(B:C = 8:20\), so \(A:B:C = 6:8:20 = 3:4:10\).

Which situation matches a negative directed number?

Answer: A bank balance of \(500\) in debt (owe \(500\)) — often modelled as \(-500\).

Explanation: Debt below zero balance is a standard directed-number model.
