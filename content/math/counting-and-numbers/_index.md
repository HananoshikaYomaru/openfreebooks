<div class="book-prose">
  <p>
    Look around you. How many birds? How many cups? You can answer by
    <strong>counting</strong> — pointing at one thing at a time until everything has been pointed at.
    This chapter uses <strong>pictures of dots</strong> to stand for real things, like marbles or
    fingers.
  </p>

  <h2 id="what-is-counting" class="book-prose__heading">What is counting?</h2>
  <p>
    To <strong>count</strong> is to touch (or point at) each object once. Say a word for each touch:
    “one, two, three…” The last word tells you <strong>how many</strong> there are.
  </p>
  <p>
    Try it on your fingers: one finger up for each thing you counted. If every object has a finger,
    the number of fingers matches the number of objects.
  </p>

  <h2 id="zero" class="book-prose__heading">Zero</h2>
  <p>
    When there is <strong>nothing</strong> to point at, we write <strong>0</strong> and say
    <strong>zero</strong>. An empty plate, no toys in the box — zero dots:
  </p>
  <p class="counting-dots counting-dots--empty" aria-label="zero dots">
    <span class="counting-dots__none">(none)</span>
  </p>
  <p>Zero comes before one when we count: first nothing, then one, then two, and so on.</p>

  <div class="book-callout" role="note">
    <p class="book-callout__label">Remember</p>
    <p>Zero means “none here,” not “forgot to count.”</p>
  </div>

  <h2 id="addition" class="book-prose__heading">Addition</h2>
  <p>
    <strong>Addition</strong> means putting two groups together. Three dots and four more dots make
    seven dots in one big group. We write <strong>3 + 4 = 7</strong>.
  </p>

  <div class="book-callout" role="note">
    <p class="book-callout__label">Adding</p>
    <p>Start with the first group, then place the second group beside it and count all the dots.</p>
  </div>

  <h2 id="multiplication" class="book-prose__heading">Multiplication</h2>
  <p>
    <strong>Multiplication</strong> is many <em>equal</em> groups. Three bags with two sweets each
    is the same as three pairs of dots. We write <strong>3 × 2 = 6</strong> — “three groups of two.”
  </p>
  <p>
    You can lay the dots in a <strong>rectangle</strong>: 3 rows with 2 dots in each row fills a
    block of 6 dots.
  </p>

  <h2 id="division" class="book-prose__heading">Division</h2>
  <p>
    <strong>Division</strong> is about <strong>sharing equally</strong> or making equal groups. If
    12 biscuits are shared among 4 friends, each friend gets the same number. We write
    <strong>12 ÷ 4 = 3</strong> — “twelve split into four equal piles gives three in each pile.”
  </p>
  <p>
    Division is the partner of multiplication. If <strong>3 × 4 = 12</strong>, then
    <strong>12 ÷ 3 = 4</strong> and <strong>12 ÷ 4 = 3</strong>. One picture of dots can answer both
    kinds of question.
  </p>

  <div class="book-callout" role="note">
    <p class="book-callout__label">Fair shares</p>
    <p>
      When every pile has the same number of dots, the share is <strong>fair</strong>. If dots are
      left over, the total does not split evenly — those extras are the <strong>remainder</strong>.
    </p>
  </div>

  <h2 id="past-ten" class="book-prose__heading">Past ten: tens and hundreds</h2>

  <h2 id="big-numbers" class="book-prose__heading">Big numbers in real life</h2>
  <p>
    The world is huge. Counting every grain of sand on a beach one by one would take forever, so we
    use <strong>big number words</strong> to talk about amounts we cannot easily picture dot by dot.
    They matter because governments, scientists, and shops all work with large totals.
  </p>

  <div class="book-table-wrap">
    <table class="book-table counting-big-table">
      <thead>
        <tr>
          <th scope="col">Word</th>
          <th scope="col">How we write it</th>
          <th scope="col">Why it matters</th>
        </tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td>hundred</td>
          <td>100</td>
          <td>About how many people fit in a big sports field; pages in a long novel.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>thousand</td>
          <td>1,000</td>
          <td>Steps you might walk in a long hike; students in a large school district.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>million</td>
          <td>1,000,000</td>
          <td>People in a large city; dollars in a national budget line item.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>billion</td>
          <td>1,000,000,000</td>
          <td>World population is several <em>billion</em>; stars in our galaxy are hundreds of billions.</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </div>

  <p>
    Each big word is <strong>one thousand times</strong> the word before (ten hundreds make a
    thousand; one thousand thousands make a million). We bundle in tens and thousands so we can
    compare and add without losing track.
  </p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>Money</strong> — prices and taxes use thousands and millions.</li>
    <li><strong>Distance</strong> — kilometres between cities add up to thousands.</li>
    <li><strong>Time</strong> — minutes in a year are in the hundreds of thousands.</li>
    <li><strong>Nature</strong> — insects in a forest, cells in your body — counts go huge fast.</li>
  </ul>

  <h2 id="history" class="book-prose__heading">History</h2>
  <p>
    Long before writing, people kept track of herds, harvests, and trade by making marks — tallies —
    one mark for each item. Archaeologists have found tally bones tens of thousands of years old.
  </p>
  <p>
    Different cultures invented symbols for amounts. The idea of <strong>zero</strong> as a number took
    time: Indian mathematicians such as <strong>Brahmagupta</strong> (7th century CE) wrote rules for
    arithmetic that included zero. The Maya used a shell sign for zero in their calendar. Zero let
    people write “none” clearly and build place-value systems later on.
  </p>
  <p>
    Multiplication grew from repeated addition in trade and land measurement — counting equal rows of
    goods or fields without adding one row at a time forever.
  </p>
  <p>
    Division appears in sharing problems as old as fair trade: split grain, land, or money into equal
    parts. Large-number names (million, billion) expanded as empires, astronomy, and finance needed to
    record bigger totals than everyday counting.
  </p>

  <h2 id="derivation" class="book-prose__heading">Derivation</h2>

  <h3 id="why-addition-works" class="book-prose__heading">Why addition combines groups</h3>
  <p>
    Suppose the first group has \(a\) objects and the second has \(b\) objects, with no overlap. If
    you put them in one pile and count from the start, you say \(a\) number words for the first
    group, then continue with \(b\) more words. The last word is the total \(a + b\). That is why
    \(3 + 4 = 7\): three objects, then four more, gives seven in all.
  </p>

  <h3 id="multiplication-as-repeated-addition" class="book-prose__heading">Multiplication as repeated addition</h3>
  <p>
    If there are \(r\) equal rows and each row has \(c\) dots, you can add
    \(c + c + \cdots + c\) (\(r\) times) to get the total. Writing \(r \times c\) saves repeating
    the same addition. For example, \(4 \times 3 = 3 + 3 + 3 + 3 = 12\).
  </p>

  <h3 id="division-as-fair-sharing" class="book-prose__heading">Division as fair sharing</h3>
  <p>
    If \(T\) dots are split into \(g\) equal piles with nothing left over, each pile has
    \(T \div g\) dots. That is the same as asking: what number times \(g\) gives \(T\)? So
    \(T \div g = q\) when \(g \times q = T\). Example: \(12 \div 4 = 3\) because \(4 \times 3 = 12\).
  </p>

  <h3 id="place-value-and-big-numbers" class="book-prose__heading">Place value and big numbers</h3>
  <p>
    In base ten, each column is worth ten times the column to its right. So \(47 = 4 \times 10 + 7\).
    Extending the pattern: \(1{,}000 = 10^3\), \(1{,}000{,}000 = 10^6\), and each “thousand step” multiplies
    by \(1{,}000\) again. That is why we group digits in threes when writing millions and billions.
  </p>

  <h2 id="applications" class="book-prose__heading">Real-life applications</h2>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Sharing snacks</strong> — \(6\) biscuits shared among \(3\) friends is \(6 \div 3 = 2\) each; check with \(3 \times 2 = 6\).</li>
    <li><strong>Rows of chairs</strong> — a room with \(4\) rows of \(5\) chairs has \(4 \times 5 = 20\) seats.</li>
    <li><strong>Egg cartons</strong> — two rows of \(5\) cups hold \(2 \times 5 = 10\) eggs.</li>
    <li><strong>Counting steps</strong> — climbing \(8\) steps then \(2\) more is \(8 + 2 = 10\) steps.</li>
    <li><strong>Population</strong> — countries and cities are counted in millions or billions.</li>
    <li><strong>Packaging</strong> — eggs in cartons (\(2 \times 5\)) and sweets split among friends (\(12 \div 4\)).</li>
  </ul>
