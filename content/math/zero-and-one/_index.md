<div class="book-prose">
  <p>
    Mathematics begins with a simple question: <strong>how many?</strong> The smallest answers are
    <strong>0</strong> and <strong>1</strong>. Before you count birds, cups, or billions, you need to
    be sure you can tell <strong>nothing</strong> from <strong>one</strong>.
  </p>

  <h2 id="zero" class="book-prose__heading">Zero — nothing</h2>
  <p>
    When there is <strong>nothing</strong> to point at, we write <strong>0</strong> and say
    <strong>zero</strong>. An empty tray, a switched-off light with no glow, silence — no dot to
    count:
  </p>
  <p class="counting-dots counting-dots--empty" aria-label="zero dots">
    <span class="counting-dots__none">(none)</span>
  </p>
  <p>
    Zero is not “forgot to look.” It means <strong>none here</strong> — the answer when the amount
    is nothing.
  </p>

  <h2 id="one" class="book-prose__heading">One — exactly one</h2>
  <p>
    When there is a single thing to point at once, we write <strong>1</strong> and say
    <strong>one</strong>. One apple on the plate, one tap, one dot:
  </p>
  <p class="counting-dots" role="img" aria-label="one dot">
    <span class="counting-dots__dot"></span>
  </p>
  <p>One is not “a few” and not “many.” It is <strong>exactly one</strong>.</p>

  <h2 id="compare" class="book-prose__heading">Tell them apart</h2>
  <p>0 and 1 sit side by side. Mixing them up changes the whole answer.</p>

  <h2 id="true-and-false" class="book-prose__heading">True and false</h2>
  <p>
    You can also ask whether a <strong>statement</strong> matches the world. “The cup is on the
    table.” — if it is there, the statement is <strong>true</strong>. If the cup is not there, the
    statement is <strong>false</strong>.
  </p>
  <p>
    For a single yes-or-no question about <strong>one</strong> thing, the same care applies:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>False</strong> fits when the answer is <strong>no</strong> — none of what we named, like <strong>0</strong>.</li>
    <li><strong>True</strong> fits when the answer is <strong>yes, exactly once</strong> — like <strong>1</strong> when we mean one clear match.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    Later you will meet longer counts and trickier logic. The habit starts here: look carefully, then
    decide — is it <strong>0 or 1</strong>? <strong>false or true</strong>?
  </p>

  <div class="book-callout" role="note">
    <p class="book-callout__label">The essence</p>
    <p>
      Telling <strong>0</strong> from <strong>1</strong> is the same kind of attention as telling
      <strong>false</strong> from <strong>true</strong>. That distinction is the foundation for every
      number and for understanding claims about the world.
    </p>
  </div>

<div class="book-prose">
  <h2 id="history" class="book-prose__heading">History</h2>
  <p>
    People counted with fingers and marks long before anyone wrote <strong>0</strong> as a number.
    In ancient India and among the Maya, thinkers found a symbol for <strong>nothing</strong> — not
    just “empty space on the page,” but a number you could calculate with.
  </p>
  <p>
    That step mattered: without zero, place value and modern arithmetic are much harder. The idea
    “nothing is something we can name” is as deep as the idea “one is one.”
  </p>
