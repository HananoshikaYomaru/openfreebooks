The problem

Textbook fees hit families hard

In many countries, families pay hundreds of dollars per child each year for printed textbooks — on top of tuition, devices, and supplies. When money is tight, students share worn copies, skip buying books, or fall behind before the term begins.

That cost is not a small inconvenience. It is a barrier to showing up prepared. Open Free Books exists so every learner can start the year with complete materials, not a receipt.

The medium

Long live the web — not the PDF

Paper textbooks are fixed: one layout, one edition, no room to fix a typo until the next print run. Most “digital” versions are the same pages exported to PDF — still static, still hard to search, still impossible to embed a simulation or a live diagram.

HTML on the open web is different. Chapters can link to sources, resize for any screen, include interactive figures, and improve over time. The book becomes something you use — not something you only carry.

People & books

Replace textbooks, not teachers

A great teacher — whether in person or, someday, assisted by thoughtful tools — still sets pace, asks hard questions, and notices when a student is stuck. That relationship is not what we are trying to automate.

Textbooks are different. They are reference and structure: explanations, exercises, diagrams. Those can be free, forkable, and kept current on the web so teachers spend less time hunting permissions and more time teaching.

Agency

Do not outsource your learning

Finishing a chapter is not the same as understanding it. Skimming for answers, copying worked solutions, or rushing to the end leaves gaps that show up on the next test — and in the years after.

Read slowly. Try the problems before you peek at hints. Sit with confusion long enough to name what you do not get. The textbook is a guide; the thinking still has to be yours. That habit is what school is really training, whether the subject is algebra or history.

Planet

Open textbooks are gentler on the Earth

Core curriculum changes slowly. Newton’s laws, the structure of a cell, and how fractions work do not need a new AI run every September. Regenerating the same chapter thousands of times burns electricity in data centers — for text that could have been written once and shared openly.

One carefully reviewed book, published on the web for everyone, means less redundant compute, less e-waste from disposable PDFs, and fewer trucks moving heavy print runs. Students get stable material they can trust; the environment gets less waste from doing the same work over and over.

Craft

Carefully crafted by brilliant people on Earth

A textbook is not a bulk download. It is years of lectures distilled, diagrams redrawn until they click, exercises tested in real classrooms, and arguments tightened by reviewers who care about getting it right.

Open Free Books gathers that work from educators and researchers at institutions around the world — people who have already spent their careers making hard ideas legible. We publish their care openly so the next student inherits the best version, not a disposable draft.
